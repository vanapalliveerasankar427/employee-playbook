PRESENCE UPGRADE CHECKLIST

Upgrade how your presence lands in rooms that matter

Strengthen the presence you bring into every room. Use this checklist to make small, deliberate shifts that help you land with calm authority instead of getting lost in noise, nerves or over-explaining.
Related Categories

When to Use This Checklist

  • Before or after high-stakes meetings where you need to be taken seriously, not just heard.
  • When you leave a room replaying what you said and worrying about how it landed.
  • When senior people join the discussion and you notice your voice, posture or structure shift.
  • When feedback hints at you being “too quiet”, “too intense”, “too detailed” or “not strategic enough”.
  • When you want your presence to match the strength of your thinking, especially under pressure.

Pro Tips

  • Treat this as a mirror, not a verdict. The goal is signal, not shame.
  • Pick one or two adjustments to test next time rather than trying to “perform” a new persona.
  • Use the same few questions or phrases repeatedly until they feel natural under pressure.
  • Notice what your body does when the stakes rise. Presence shifts start with regulation, not scripts.
  • Revisit every few weeks so you’re tracking progress, not chasing perfection.

Your Presence Upgrade Checklist

In high-stakes meetings, I speak at a pace that is calm and measured rather than rushed or hesitant.
I can state my key point in one clear sentence before adding background or detail.
My posture is grounded and open (feet, shoulders, eye line) rather than collapsed, defensive or withdrawn.
I allow short pauses so my points land, instead of filling silence with extra words or caveats.
When challenged, I respond to the substance of the point rather than defending myself personally.
Before important conversations, I can name the outcome I want from the discussion in one line.
My facial expressions broadly match what I am saying (e.g. I am not smiling while delivering serious feedback).
I have at least one question I can use to slow reactive conversations and bring people back to the real issue.
I notice when my heart rate spikes or breathing tightens and know one simple way to steady myself.
On remote calls, I deliberately adjust camera framing, lighting and sound so I feel and look present, not faded out.
I do not noticeably shrink my voice or over-explain when senior people join the room.
After important meetings, I take two minutes to note what landed well and what I would adjust next time.
