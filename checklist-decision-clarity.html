When to Use This Checklist

  • When you're stuck between options and the "right answer" keeps shifting depending on who speaks last.
  • When decisions are being made without a clear owner, criteria, or definition of success.
  • When you feel pressure to choose quickly â€” but you know the downside will land on you later.
  • When stakeholders want different outcomes and you need a decision that survives scrutiny.
  • When you need to recommend a move to senior people without over-explaining or hand-waving risk.

Pro Tips

  • If you can't write the decision in one sentence, you don't have a decision yet â€” you have a topic.
  • Strong decisions are rarely "perfect". They're clear, bounded, and revisitable under defined conditions.
  • Trade-offs don't disappear. They just show up later as cost, risk, rework, or reputation damage.
  • Pre-wire the decision: speak to the 1â€“2 key stakeholders before the meeting so you don't get blindsided live.
  • Decision clarity isn't just logic â€” it's ownership + criteria + a clean next step people can execute.

Your Decision Clarity Checklist

I can write the decision in one sentence with a clear verb (approve / choose / stop / start / prioritise).
I know what "good" looks like: success criteria, constraints, and what we will not compromise.
I'm clear why this decision matters now (and what happens if we delay or do nothing).
I've separated facts from assumptions â€” and I can name the top assumptions driving the decision.
I have at least two realistic options (including "do nothing" if that is truly on the table).
I can explain the trade-offs clearly (cost / time / risk / people impact) without hiding the downside.
I know who owns the decision and what the decision-making path is (recommend / decide / approve).
I've identified the key stakeholders who can block, slow, or accelerate the decision â€” and what they care about.
I've named the top risks (including reputational/political risk) and what would make each risk more likely.
I've set guardrails and thresholds (what "good enough" looks like, and what triggers a rethink later).
I understand the downstream work created by the decision (dependencies, handoffs, and who absorbs the load).
I can communicate the decision cleanly: what we decided, why, what changes, and what happens next.
