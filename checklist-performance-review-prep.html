PERFORMANCE REVIEW PREP CHECKLIST

Walk into your performance review with leverage, not hope

Performance reviews are rarely “about performance” in a pure sense — they’re about narrative, evidence, calibration, and whether your impact is easy for someone to reward. Use this checklist to turn your work into a clean case: outcomes, proof, alignment, and the ask.

When to Use This Checklist

  • 2–3 weeks before your performance review so you can gather proof (not rely on memory).
  • When you suspect “calibration” will be used to keep ratings or pay increases flat.
  • When your manager is supportive but busy — you need to make your case easy to champion.
  • When your role is ambiguous and you need to define impact and scope in your own words.
  • Before a promotion conversation or pay review so you avoid a vague “let’s see next cycle”.

Pro Tips

  • Reviews reward what’s legible. Your job is to make impact obvious, not merely true.
  • Do not submit a list of tasks. Submit outcomes, proof, and “why it mattered”.
  • Assume the decision is partly made in a separate room. Write for the room you’re not in.
  • Make your ask clean: rating, scope, pay, title, or development — pick the primary one.
  • Be hard on evidence, soft on tone. Calm, specific, and measurable wins.

Your Performance Review Prep Checklist

I know the review format, timelines, rating criteria, and who influences the final outcome (manager, calibration panel, HR).
I have a single evidence pack: outcomes, metrics, emails/Slack praise, stakeholder feedback, and delivery proof in one place.
I can name my 3–5 highest-value outcomes this cycle (not tasks) and explain the business impact clearly.
I have mapped my outcomes to team/company priorities, OKRs, or “what leadership cared about” this period.
Where metrics exist, I’ve quantified impact (time saved, risk reduced, revenue protected/grown, quality improved).
I have at least 2–4 stakeholder signals (cross-functional, customer, senior) to support my impact narrative.
I have identified likely critique areas (misses, delays, conflict) and prepared a calm, factual framing with learning.
I have a development plan: what I want to grow, what opportunities I need, and what “good next cycle” looks like.
I have anticipated calibration dynamics: budget limits, forced distribution, manager caution, and how they may show up.
I have defined my primary ask (rating, pay, promotion, scope) and a credible secondary ask if the primary is blocked.
I have made it easy for my manager to advocate: a 1-page summary they can reuse in calibration without rewriting.
I’ve rehearsed my narrative (2 minutes), my evidence (30 seconds), and my ask (1 sentence) — including on remote calls.
