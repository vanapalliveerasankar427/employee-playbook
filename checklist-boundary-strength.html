BOUNDARY STRENGTH CHECKLIST

Identify weak points causing overwhelm and reclaim control

When you feel overwhelmed, stretched too thin, or unable to say no, use this checklist to identify exactly where your boundaries are weakest and take targeted action to strengthen them.
Explore Categories
Clarity, Priorities & Direction Manager Relationship & Leadership Style Workplace Dynamics & Politics

When to Use This Checklist

  • When you feel overwhelmed, stretched too thin, or burned out
  • When you struggle to say "no" to additional requests or tasks
  • When work regularly spills into personal/family time
  • When you feel responsible for solving other people's problems
  • When you feel guilty for taking breaks or time off
  • When you notice resentment building toward colleagues or work

Pro Tips

  • Be honest with yourself — boundary weakness is not a failure, it’s an opportunity
  • Boundaries protect your energy, focus, and wellbeing — they’re not selfish
  • Start small — strengthen one boundary at a time rather than trying to fix everything
  • Communicate boundaries clearly and consistently — people can’t respect what they don’t know
  • Remember: “No” is a complete sentence. You don’t need to justify or over-explain

Your Boundary Strength Checklist

I have clear start and end times for my workday and stick to them consistently.
I take my full lunch break away from my desk most days.
I don't feel obligated to respond to work messages outside of work hours.
I can confidently say "no" to requests that don't align with my priorities or capacity.
I don't take on other people's work or responsibilities to avoid conflict.
I don't feel responsible for other people's emotional reactions or problems.
I can redirect conversations that become unproductive or emotionally draining.
I protect focused work time by declining unnecessary meetings or scheduling "do not disturb" blocks.
I communicate clearly when my plate is full and negotiate deadlines or scope when needed.
I don't feel guilty for taking time off or using my entitled leave.
I can assertively communicate my needs without apologising excessively.
I have strategies to manage work-related stress that don't involve overworking.
